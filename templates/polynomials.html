03 // POLYNOMIAL FUNCTIONS: STRUCTURE AND ROOTS

In Algebra II, polynomial functions are the bedrock for advanced problem-solving. A polynomial is defined by the form: $$P(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0$$ where $n$ is a non-negative integer (the **degree**) and the coefficients ($a_i$) are real numbers.

KEY TERMINOLOGY

  • **Degree ($n$):** The highest power of $x$. This determines the maximum number of roots and the end behavior.
  • **Leading Coefficient ($a_n$):** The coefficient of the term with the highest degree. This heavily influences the graph's direction.
  • **Root / Zero:** An $x$-value where $P(x) = 0$. Graphically, these are the $x$-intercepts.

FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF ALGEBRA (FTofA)

This is the most critical concept: **A polynomial of degree $n$ has exactly $n$ roots in the complex number system.** This means a cubic polynomial (degree 3) has exactly three roots, which may be real, imaginary, or a combination thereof.

PRACTICE 3.1: DEGREE & ROOTS

A polynomial $P(x) = 5x^4 - 2x^3 + 7x - 1$ has a degree of $\mathbf{4}$. Therefore, it must have exactly $\mathbf{4}$ roots.

What is the leading coefficient? $\mathbf{5}$

RATIONAL ROOT THEOREM (RRT)

The RRT provides a systematic way to find all possible **rational** roots ($\frac{p}{q}$) of a polynomial with integer coefficients. This is crucial when factoring higher-degree polynomials.

THE RRT FORMULA

$$\text{Possible Rational Roots} = \frac{\text{Factors of Constant Term } (p)}{\text{Factors of Leading Coefficient } (q)}$$

PRACTICE 3.2: APPLYING RRT

Consider $P(x) = 2x^3 + 3x^2 - 8x + 3$.

  • **$p$ (Factors of Constant term 3):** $\pm 1, \pm 3$
  • **$q$ (Factors of Leading Coefficient 2):** $\pm 1, \pm 2$

What are the possible rational roots ($\frac{p}{q}$)? $\mathbf{\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm 1/2, \pm 3/2}$

THE REMAINDER AND FACTOR THEOREMS

  • **Remainder Theorem:** If a polynomial $P(x)$ is divided by $(x - c)$, the remainder is $P(c)$. This is used to quickly check if a possible root is a true root.
  • **Factor Theorem:** A binomial $(x - c)$ is a factor of the polynomial $P(x)$ if and only if $P(c) = 0$. (i.e., $c$ is a root).

PRACTICE 3.3: USING THE FACTOR THEOREM

Use the Factor Theorem to determine if $(x - 1)$ is a factor of $P(x) = x^3 - 4x^2 + 5x - 2$.

You need to calculate $P(1)$:

$$P(1) = (1)^3 - 4(1)^2 + 5(1) - 2$$ $$P(1) = 1 - 4 + 5 - 2 = \mathbf{0}$$

Since $P(1) = 0$, $\mathbf{(x - 1)}$ is indeed a factor.
