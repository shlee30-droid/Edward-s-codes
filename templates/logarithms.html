02 // LOGARITHMS: THE INVERSE OPERATION

Logarithms are the inverse of exponential functions. They answer the question: **"To what power must the base be raised to get a certain number?"** They are essential for solving equations where the variable is in the exponent.

LOG/EXPONENTIAL EQUIVALENCE

The core relationship that defines a logarithm is the ability to switch between exponential and logarithmic form. This is your primary tool for solving these functions.

EQUIVALENCE

$$\text{If } b^x = y, \text{ then } \log_b(y) = x$$

Where $b$ is the base ($b > 0$ and $b \neq 1$), $x$ is the exponent/logarithm, and $y$ is the argument.

KEY PROPERTIES (THE LAWS OF LOGS)

Logarithms have three fundamental rules derived from the laws of exponents. These are used to expand or condense logarithmic expressions.

1. PRODUCT RULE (Addition)

$$\log_b(xy) = \log_b(x) + \log_b(y)$$

2. QUOTIENT RULE (Subtraction)

$$\log_b\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b(x) - \log_b(y)$$

3. POWER RULE (Multiplication)

$$\log_b(x^p) = p \cdot \log_b(x)$$

COMMON LOGS

We typically work with two special bases:

  • Common Log: Base 10 ($\log_{10}(x)$ or just $\log(x)$). Used in science and engineering.
  • Natural Log: Base $e$ ($\log_e(x)$ or $\ln(x)$). Used extensively in calculus and financial modeling. The constant $e \approx 2.718$.

CHANGE OF BASE FORMULA

To calculate a logarithm with an unfamiliar base using a standard calculator:

$$\log_b(x) = \frac{\log(x)}{\log(b)}$$
